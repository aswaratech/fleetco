# ADR-0037: Preventive-maintenance and service-scheduling aggregate — a dedicated `ServiceSchedule` + `ServiceRecord` pair, anchored to the central `Vehicle`, with intervals expressed in km / engine-hours / calendar, a "service due / overdue" badge that rotates the compliance pattern, and a cost link into the existing `ExpenseLog` ledger

- **Status:** Proposed
- **Date:** 2026-06-18
- **Decider:** Product owner (CEO)

## Context

FleetCo records what maintenance **cost** but cannot say when maintenance is **due**. Today, when a truck gets an oil change or an excavator has its hydraulic filters replaced, the operator records an `ExpenseLog` row with `category = MAINTENANCE` (or `REPAIR`) and an `amountPaisa` — a backward-looking ledger entry made *after the work is done*. That ledger is real and useful (it feeds the per-vehicle cost report, ADR's Reports v1), but it is **purely reactive**: there is no stored "next service due" signal anywhere in the system, so nothing reminds the operator that the excavator is approaching its 250-engine-hour service or that the tipper is 800 km past its oil change. Maintenance happens when something breaks or when the operator happens to remember — which, for a one-person company running a heavy fleet, is exactly the failure mode preventive maintenance exists to prevent.

The problem is sharpened by the fact that **the fleet meters three different ways**, and a scheduler has to speak all three:

- **Trucks and tippers are serviced on kilometers.** A 5,000–10,000 km oil change, a tire rotation every N km — distance intervals against the odometer the system has tracked since iter 1 (`Vehicle.odometerCurrentKm`).
- **Earthmoving equipment is serviced on engine-hours.** An excavator's 250-hour oil/filter service, a 500-hour hydraulic service — hour intervals that have **no relation** to distance (the machine barely moves while its engine runs hard). This dimension did not exist in the schema until **ADR-0036** proposed the engine-hours columns (`Vehicle.engineHoursCurrent`, integer tenths of an hour) precisely so that this kind of scheduling becomes possible. ADR-0036 names this ADR (DEC2/ADR-0037) as the consumer of the engine-hours trigger it provides.
- **Some service is purely calendar-based.** An annual inspection, a quarterly greasing, a battery replacement every two years — intervals measured in days against the wall clock, independent of how far or how long the asset has run.

FleetCo can schedule **none** of these today. That is the gap this ADR closes.

Three pieces of machinery already in the repo make this cheap to build, and the recommendation is to compose them rather than invent anything new:

- **The compliance due/overdue pattern (ADR-0031, `apps/web/src/lib/compliance.ts`).** `complianceBadgeState(expiryIso, now, windowDays = 30)` is a pure helper that classifies a stored date against "now" into `none` / `expired` / `expiring-soon` / `ok`, using the load-bearing **UTC-calendar-day rule** so the window boundary is timezone-deterministic. The Vehicle detail page already paints its output as an amber "Expiring soon" / red "Expired" `<Badge>` (`ComplianceExpiry` in `apps/web/src/app/vehicles/[id]/page.tsx`), and `worstComplianceState` rolls several dates into a single worst-of badge for the list/dashboard. "Service due soon / overdue" is the **same shape** — a threshold classification surfaced as a glanceable badge — and the calendar-interval case is *literally the same computation* (a date vs. now). This is the pattern to rotate, exactly as ADR-0036 rotated the odometer machinery for engine-hours.
- **The `ExpenseLog` MAINTENANCE/REPAIR ledger (`apps/api/prisma/schema.prisma`).** `ExpenseLog` already carries the authoritative cost of maintenance: a nullable `vehicleId` FK, a `category` (`ExpenseCategory` enum — `MAINTENANCE` / `REPAIR` are two of its eight values), an authoritative `amountPaisa` (operator-entered, not derived), and `vendor` / `receiptNumber` / `notes`. A service-history record must **reference** this cost, never duplicate it — there must be exactly one money column for a maintenance spend, and it already exists.
- **The Trip→Vehicle auto-update + the aggregate CRUD pattern (iters 11–13, ADR-0006).** The odometer machinery advances `Vehicle.odometerCurrentKm` to a completed trip's reading **inside one Prisma `$transaction`**, under a monotonic "once forward, stays forward" rule (ADR-0036 §B rotates this for hours). Completing a service is the same kind of event: it advances a schedule's "last service" anchor forward in one transaction. And every Phase-1 aggregate (`customers/`, `geofences/` per ADR-0030) follows the same vertical-slice shape — `model` + migration → service → controller (`AuthGuard`, Zod `.strict()`, the `{ items, total, skip, take, sortBy, sortDir }` list shape, `createdById` from the session, standardized P2002/P2003/P2025 error mapping) → tests against real Postgres → web pages + a nav button.

This ADR is required for the reasons CLAUDE.md step 4 enumerates. It introduces a **new data model** (two aggregates), it **anchors on the central `Vehicle` aggregate** (ADR-0003) and references the `Trip`-adjacent `ExpenseLog` ledger, it adds a cross-cutting "due/overdue" classification surface, **and** it pulls a later phase forward (see below). Per the project's settled practice — ADR-0029 fixed telematics storage before the GPS code, ADR-0030 fixed the geofence aggregate before the geofence code, ADR-0036 fixed the engine-hours shape before the engine-hours code — the confirmation vehicle is an ADR. **This ADR decides shape and policy only. It writes no code, no `schema.prisma` edit, no migration, and adds no dependency.** Those land with the implementing slice (Program B's preventive-maintenance ticket) once this ADR is accepted.

### Phase mismatch and the ADR-0036 dependency (stated honestly)

This is **not** Phase-2 scope, and a fresh reader must see two facts plainly before the PO weighs the pull-forward:

1. **This proposes Phase-3 / Program-B work ahead of its phase.** Preventive maintenance for the equipment half of the fleet is **Phase-3 headline material** (the roadmap's "Program B"), the natural sibling of ADR-0036's engine-hours dimension. It is proposed here under the **ADR-0025 ahead-of-gate pattern** — the documented-exception mechanism that opened Phase 2 before Phase 1 reached daily use. But ADR-0025 was explicitly a *one-time* exception scoped to the Phase-1 → Phase-2 transition; pulling Phase-3 scope forward is a **new** exception that needs its own deliberate decision. This ADR is where that proposal is recorded; accepting it is what authorizes the pull-forward. An agent must never self-accept this ADR, add an `## Acceptance` block, or flip the status — the PO ratifies separately.

2. **The first production deploy is still owed.** Phase 1 is content-complete but **not in daily use** — the deploy, the restore-from-backup drill, live SLI/DORA reporting, and the retrospective sign-off all remain open (`docs/CURRENT_PHASE.md`; ADR-0025 commitment 1). Every model added before the deploy widens the "built-but-unused" surface ADR-0025 flagged as its central risk: code shipped against assumption, never exercised by a real operator. Two new aggregates — sized against a guess about how the operator will define intervals and record services in the field, not against observed use — are a real cost while the app has never run in production.

3. **This ADR depends on ADR-0036, which is itself only `Proposed`.** The engine-hours interval — the dimension that makes scheduling *equipment* possible, and the whole reason this is worth pulling forward — does not exist in the schema until ADR-0036 is accepted **and** its implementing slice ships the `meterType` discriminator and the nullable `engineHoursCurrent` column. The km and calendar interval types of this aggregate do not need ADR-0036; only `ENGINE_HOURS` does. So the honest ordering constraint is: **accepting and building ADR-0037 ahead of ADR-0036 would ship a maintenance scheduler blind to exactly the half of the fleet (earthmoving equipment) that most needs scheduled service.** This ADR should be accepted *after* (or together with) ADR-0036, and its implementing slice sequenced after ADR-0036's, or else scoped to km+calendar only with the engine-hours dimension following ADR-0036's code. The dependency is called out in the Decision (commitment 3), the Consequences, and "Revisit when".

The honest framing, as with ADR-0036: **this ADR proposes the pull-forward; it does not assume it.** The PO decides at acceptance whether to (a) accept and let Program B build the PM slice now (after/with ADR-0036), (b) accept but hold the implementing slice until the deploy lands or Phase 3 opens, or (c) reject the pull-forward and keep this as a `Proposed` record for when Phase 3 arrives.

## Decision

**Model preventive maintenance as a dedicated two-table aggregate anchored on the central `Vehicle` — a `ServiceSchedule` (a recurring maintenance interval for a vehicle) and a `ServiceRecord` (a completed service event, the history) — rather than as columns on `Vehicle`; express every interval as one `intervalType` discriminator (`DISTANCE_KM` / `ENGINE_HOURS` / `CALENDAR_DAYS`) plus one integer `intervalValue` in that dimension's minor units (km, tenths-of-an-hour per ADR-0036, or days), with a denormalized "last service" anchor on the schedule from which "next due" is derived; advance that anchor forward when a `ServiceRecord` is recorded, inside the same Prisma `$transaction` as the record write, mirroring the Trip→Vehicle odometer bump; link each `ServiceRecord` to its cost by a nullable FK to an existing `ExpenseLog` MAINTENANCE/REPAIR row — never a duplicated money column; and surface "service due soon / overdue" by rotating the `complianceBadgeState` pattern into a sibling helper that classifies a schedule against the vehicle's current meter reading (or the wall clock for calendar intervals) and paints the same amber/red `<Badge>` the compliance section already uses.** Eight commitments define the shape, grouped. As with its sibling decision-records (ADR-0030, ADR-0036), this ADR **recommends** and leaves the genuinely owner-level picks — the storage shape, the per-dimension "due soon" windows, and the phase pull-forward — to the PO at acceptance.

### A. The aggregate shape — two tables, not columns on `Vehicle`

1. **A dedicated `ServiceSchedule` + `ServiceRecord` aggregate, anchored to `Vehicle` by FK (recommended; owner-level pick).** A vehicle has **many** schedules at once — an excavator might run a 250-hour engine service, a 500-hour hydraulic service, and an annual inspection simultaneously — and an **unbounded, growing** history of completed services. That is a one-to-many (and one-to-many) shape, which columns on `Vehicle` cannot express; you would need a fixed number of "service slots," which is the wrong cardinality. This is the deliberate contrast with **ADR-0036**, which *did* put engine-hours columns directly on `Vehicle`: the current hour-meter reading is a single, cardinality-one fact per vehicle (like the odometer), whereas service schedules and history are cardinality-N. The dedicated-aggregate choice mirrors ADR-0030's reasoning (a first-class `Geofence` aggregate over columns) and the Phase-1 aggregate CRUD convention. Both tables carry a `vehicleId` FK with `onDelete: Restrict` (the house posture — `FuelLog`, `ExpenseLog`, `GpsPing` all Restrict), so a vehicle with maintenance history cannot be silently deleted; the existing `VehiclesService.delete` `P2003 → 409` arm covers the new referencer with no change. The two new aggregates are the **only** schema additions — `Vehicle` itself is untouched (it is the anchor, not the carrier).

### B. The interval representation — the three meter dimensions, one discriminator

2. **One `intervalType` enum + one integer `intervalValue`, in that dimension's minor units.** A `ServiceSchedule` stores an `intervalType` enum — recommended values **`DISTANCE_KM`** (trucks/tippers, against `Vehicle.odometerCurrentKm`), **`ENGINE_HOURS`** (equipment, against ADR-0036's `Vehicle.engineHoursCurrent`), and **`CALENDAR_DAYS`** (annual/periodic, against the wall clock) — plus a single integer `intervalValue` whose **unit is fixed by the type**: kilometers for `DISTANCE_KM`, **integer tenths of an hour** for `ENGINE_HOURS` (so a 250-hour interval stores as `2500`, byte-for-byte the ADR-0036 storage convention, never a float), and days for `CALENDAR_DAYS`. This is the integer-minor-units rule (`paisa`, `litersMl`, the ADR-0036 hours) applied to intervals; cross-field math stays in integers, display divides where needed. One discriminated column-pair is preferred over three nullable type-specific columns because it is unambiguous and indexable, and the closed enum makes an impossible type a compile error — the same reasoning ADR-0036 used for `meterType` and ADR-0030 used for `GeofenceType`.

3. **A meter-consistency rule binds `intervalType` to the vehicle's `meterType` (the ADR-0036 dependency, made a validation).** An `ENGINE_HOURS` schedule is only meaningful on a vehicle whose `meterType` is `ENGINE_HOURS` or `BOTH` (ADR-0036) — it needs `engineHoursCurrent` to measure against — so the service layer rejects an `ENGINE_HOURS` schedule on an `ODOMETER_KM`-only vehicle with a clean 400, re-validated against the merged shape on update, mirroring ADR-0030's type/ownership `superRefine` and ADR-0036's meter-aware trip validation. `DISTANCE_KM` is valid on any vehicle (every vehicle has an odometer). `CALENDAR_DAYS` is valid on any vehicle (it needs no meter at all). **This is precisely where the ADR-0036 dependency bites:** until ADR-0036's `meterType` + `engineHoursCurrent` ship, the `ENGINE_HOURS` branch has nothing to validate against or measure against, which is why this ADR is sequenced after ADR-0036 (see "Phase mismatch").

### C. The anchor and the "next due" derivation — reuse the odometer-bump machinery

4. **A denormalized "last service" anchor on the schedule, from which "next due" is derived.** Each `ServiceSchedule` stores its last-service anchor as a small snapshot: `lastServiceAt` (DateTime), and the meter reading captured at that service — `lastServiceOdometerKm` (Int?) and/or `lastServiceEngineHours` (Int?, tenths). "Next due" is **derived, not stored**: for `DISTANCE_KM`, `nextDueKm = lastServiceOdometerKm + intervalValue`; for `ENGINE_HOURS`, `nextDueHours = lastServiceEngineHours + intervalValue`; for `CALENDAR_DAYS`, `nextDueDate = lastServiceAt + intervalValue days`. Denormalizing the anchor (rather than computing it from a "latest `ServiceRecord`" subquery on every render and every reminder scan) follows the **same denormalization** `Vehicle.odometerCurrentKm` already makes — it stores the latest completed trip's reading on the vehicle rather than deriving it each time. The anchor is seeded at schedule creation from the vehicle's current readings (or operator-supplied "last serviced at / last serviced odometer-or-hours" values, for an asset already mid-cycle when first entered).

5. **Recording a `ServiceRecord` advances the schedule's anchor inside one `$transaction`.** When the operator records a completed service against a schedule, the implementing slice writes the `ServiceRecord` **and** advances that schedule's `lastServiceAt` + `lastService*` anchor to the record's values **in the same Prisma interactive `$transaction`** — the exact shape of the Trip→Vehicle odometer bump (iter 11), so a mid-flight failure can never leave a recorded service with a stale schedule anchor. Recording a service therefore resets "next due" forward by one interval. The compensating action for a wrong entry is the documented manual-correction path (edit the schedule's anchor or the record), identical to the odometer correction story. A "once forward, stays forward" guard mirrors the odometer's where it applies (a backdated correction record should not move a meter anchor backward); calendar anchors take the recorded date.

### D. The cost link — reference `ExpenseLog`, never duplicate the money

6. **A `ServiceRecord` references its cost by a nullable FK to an `ExpenseLog` row; it carries no `amountPaisa`.** The authoritative cost of a maintenance spend lives in exactly one place — an `ExpenseLog` row with `category` `MAINTENANCE` or `REPAIR` and its `amountPaisa` — and a `ServiceRecord` **references** that row via a nullable `expenseLogId` FK rather than copying the amount. The FK lives on the **new** `ServiceRecord` (pointing at the shipped `ExpenseLog`), so `ExpenseLog` is untouched except for the back-relation field Prisma requires on both sides of a relation — a model edit, **not** a data column or a migration on existing rows. The FK is **nullable** because (a) a warranty service has no cost, and (b) a service may be recorded before its invoice is keyed in; it is `onDelete: Restrict` for house consistency (an expense linked to a service record cannot be silently deleted out from under it — `SetNull` is the noted alternative if the PO prefers the service-record-survives-cost-deletion posture). Two boundaries keep this clean: a `ServiceRecord` whose `expenseLogId` is set should point at a `MAINTENANCE`/`REPAIR` row whose `vehicleId` matches the record's vehicle (a service-layer consistency check, the rotation of the Fuel/Expense trip-vehicle check); and the maintenance module reads cost **through the `ExpenseLog` service interface**, not by reaching into its repository — the cross-module rule (CLAUDE.md) is about service-code access, and a Prisma FK is the same first-class relationship every aggregate already uses to reference `Vehicle`/`Trip`/`Customer`. The per-vehicle maintenance-cost report continues to read `ExpenseLog` directly (unchanged); the `ServiceRecord` link is additive context ("this `MAINTENANCE` expense was the 250-hour service on schedule X"), never a second source of the number.

### E. The badge — rotate the compliance due/overdue pattern

7. **A pure `serviceScheduleState` helper, the sibling of `complianceBadgeState`, painting the same amber/red `<Badge>`.** "Service due soon / overdue" is surfaced exactly as compliance lapses are: a pure classifier returning the same four-state union (`none` / `overdue` / `due-soon` / `ok`) feeding the shipped `<Badge>` (`error` = red "Service overdue", `warning` = amber "Service due soon"), so **no new design token is introduced and the drift test stays green** (the ADR-0031 N3 posture). The honest reuse boundary, stated so the implementing agent gets it right:
   - **The `CALENDAR_DAYS` case *is* `complianceBadgeState`** — a stored date (`nextDueDate`) compared against `now` with a due-soon window in days, under the identical UTC-calendar-day rule. The implementing slice reuses the shipped helper (or a thin re-export), it does not re-derive date math.
   - **The `DISTANCE_KM` / `ENGINE_HOURS` cases reuse the *shape*, generalized from "days remaining" to "units remaining":** `remaining = nextDue − vehicle.<currentReading>`; `remaining < 0` → `overdue`, `0 ≤ remaining ≤ window` → `due-soon`, else `ok`. The window is in the same units (km or tenths-of-hour). This is a small generalization of the compliance primitive — same state machine, same labels-paired-with-hue discipline, a different measuring stick — and is best implemented by extracting the shared four-state core so both the date-specialized and meter-specialized helpers sit on one primitive.
   - **A null current reading yields `none`,** exactly as a null compliance expiry does: an `ENGINE_HOURS` schedule on a vehicle whose `engineHoursCurrent` is still null (ADR-0036: null until a reading is keyed) shows the schedule with no due/overdue badge yet — "tracks hours" and "has an hours reading yet" are different facts, the same distinction ADR-0036 draws.
   The badge surfaces per-schedule in a new "Service schedules" `<section>` on the Vehicle detail page (mirroring the "Compliance" section), and a `worstServiceState` roll-up — the rotation of `worstComplianceState` — can paint a single worst-of badge on the vehicles list and the Home dashboard when wanted (a separable follow-up, not v1, exactly as the compliance roll-up was).

### F. Scope boundaries — what this ADR does not decide

8. **Out of scope, stated so the boundaries are clean.** This ADR does **not** decide: (a) **reminder *delivery*** — email/SMS/push notification when a service crosses into due-soon/overdue is **DEC3/ADR-0038** (the email/notification infrastructure + the "reminder delivery" SLI), Phase 3; this aggregate is the passive *producer* of "service due/overdue" that the reminder channel will read, the same relationship the compliance badge has to its Phase-3 delivery (ADR-0031 §F). The badge here is a passive indicator on a page the operator already opens — no cron, no queue, no notification. (b) **A `ServiceType` enum** — services are open-ended and manufacturer-specific (unlike `ExpenseCategory`'s closed cost-bucket set), so the recommendation is a free-text `name` on `ServiceSchedule` (mirroring `Job.description`), with an enum revisited only if buckets emerge. (c) **A parts/inventory model**, labor-hours, or technician assignment — this is interval scheduling + cost-linked history, not a full CMMS. (d) **Automated odometer/hour-meter-driven schedule evaluation as a background scan** — the badge computes on-demand at render in v1 (like compliance); a daily "scan all schedules and emit due/overdue" job belongs with DEC3's delivery channel, not here. (e) **Multi-vehicle / fleet-template schedules** (define once, apply to many like-model assets) — a useful future ergonomic, but v1 is per-vehicle schedules. (f) A **`ScheduleStatus` (ACTIVE / INACTIVE)** to pause a schedule without deleting it is *recommended* (mirroring `CustomerStatus`) but its exact shape is an implementing-slice detail.

## Alternatives considered

**Schedule + anchor columns on `Vehicle` (no dedicated aggregate).** Add a fixed set of "next service due" columns directly to `Vehicle`, the way the iter-14 compliance columns and ADR-0036's engine-hours columns sit on the vehicle. Rejected as the recommendation because the cardinality is wrong: a vehicle has *many* concurrent schedules (engine, hydraulic, calendar inspection) and an *unbounded growing* history, neither of which a fixed column set can hold — you would invent "service slot 1..N" columns, the classic wrong-shape. Columns work for compliance and engine-hours precisely because those are *fixed, cardinality-one* facts (three named documents; one current hour-meter); maintenance schedules and history are cardinality-N, which is a table. This is the deliberate, explicitly-drawn contrast with ADR-0036.

**A generic "reminder" / rules engine** (store arbitrary "remind me when condition X holds" rules over any field). Rejected as over-engineering — it is the entity-attribute-value / rules-engine anti-pattern applied to what are two well-known, fixed dimensions (a meter threshold and a calendar interval), the same reason ADR-0036 rejected a polymorphic `meterReading` table. Two typed interval columns are simpler, indexable, mirror the proven compliance/odometer shapes, and need no rule interpreter. A general reminder engine, if ever wanted, is a much later and separately-justified thing; preventive maintenance does not need it.

**Reuse `ExpenseLog` MAINTENANCE/REPAIR rows directly as the service history (no `ServiceRecord` table).** Treat the existing maintenance expenses *as* the history and hang the schedule anchor off "the latest MAINTENANCE expense." Rejected: an `ExpenseLog` row has **no meter reading** (no odometer/hours captured at service time), so it cannot advance a meter-based schedule's anchor — the core mechanic of the feature; not every service has a cost row (warranty work) and not every `MAINTENANCE` expense is a *scheduled* service (a one-off breakdown repair), so the schedule↔completion link has nowhere honest to live; and overloading the cost ledger with scheduling state couples two concerns that drift independently. The recommended split — a `ServiceRecord` that owns the service facts (meter reading, schedule link, date) and **references** an `ExpenseLog` for the money (commitment 6) — keeps each table about one thing and keeps the single money column where it already is.

**A separate `*Hours`-style parallel without the `intervalType` discriminator** (three nullable interval columns: `intervalKm?`, `intervalHours?`, `intervalDays?`, relying on which-is-non-null to mean the type). Rejected for the same reason ADR-0036 rejected discriminator-free nullable columns: it cannot cleanly express "exactly one interval dimension per schedule," invites the half-populated row, and pushes the type inference onto every read site. One `intervalType` enum + one `intervalValue` is unambiguous and a typo is a compile error.

**One unified `Service` table (schedule and history collapsed into rows with a "is this a plan or a completion?" flag).** Rejected: a schedule (a recurring rule with an interval and a moving anchor) and a record (an immutable completed event) have different lifecycles, different mutability, and different fields; collapsing them forces nullable columns that are meaningful for one row-kind and meaningless for the other, and muddies the "advance the anchor on completion" transaction. Two tables with a clean FK between them is the honest shape (and the ADR-0030 precedent for typed aggregates).

**Devanagari / Bikram-Sambat-native interval input, or a fourth interval dimension now** (operating cycles, liters pumped). Rejected as not warranted for v1 — calendar intervals render and compare in the existing BS-aware date pipeline (ADR-0031) on the display side while staying ISO/UTC in storage, and km/hours are the two meter dimensions the fleet actually runs on. A third *meter* dimension is named in "Revisit when" if an asset ever needs it, exactly as ADR-0036 parked the same question.

**Build the preventive-maintenance schema now without a separate ADR.** Rejected on CLAUDE.md step 4: two new aggregates anchored on the central `Vehicle`, a new cross-cutting due/overdue classification, a dependency on a not-yet-accepted ADR, and a Phase-3 pull-forward are exactly the changes that warrant PO confirmation before code. The ADR is that gate, mirroring ADR-0029 (storage before GPS code), ADR-0030 (aggregate before geofence code), and ADR-0036 (shape before engine-hours code).

## Consequences

### What this makes easier

Maintenance stops being something the operator has to remember. Every asset — the km-metered trucks, the **hour-metered equipment that was invisible until ADR-0036**, and the calendar-driven inspections — gets a stored "next service due" and a glanceable amber/red badge the moment it enters its due-soon window or goes overdue, surfaced on the Vehicle detail page exactly where the compliance badges already are. Because the design is "the compliance pattern and the odometer bump, rotated," the implementing slice is mostly composition of proven parts: the badge reuses `complianceBadgeState`'s state machine and `<Badge>`, the anchor-advance reuses the Trip→Vehicle `$transaction`, the CRUD reuses the Phase-1 aggregate convention, and the cost is the `ExpenseLog` row that already exists. The history-with-cost-link turns the per-vehicle cost report from "what did we spend" into "what did we spend, on which scheduled service," without adding a second money column. And it is legible memory: one ADR explains that FleetCo schedules maintenance three ways, why the schedule/history is a dedicated aggregate rather than columns, and why a service references its cost rather than restating it.

### What this makes harder

The schema grows two aggregates and a new standing invariant the maintenance flow must honor: a schedule's `intervalType` must stay consistent with the vehicle's `meterType`, and recording a service must advance the anchor transactionally or the "next due" silently rots. The due/overdue classification is now a third place (after compliance and, prospectively, anything else) that must respect the UTC-calendar-day rule for its calendar case and the integer-minor-units rule for its meter cases — a contributor who computes "days until due" in local time or "hours remaining" as a float reintroduces a bug the pattern was built to avoid. The cost link adds a referential edge (`ServiceRecord → ExpenseLog`) and a small consistency check (matching vehicle, MAINTENANCE/REPAIR category) that every service-recording path must keep honoring.

### Costs we accept

- **Two new aggregates set without production telemetry, on top of an undeployed Phase 1 (ADR-0025).** The interval shapes, the per-dimension due-soon windows, and the recording UX are sized against assumption about how a Nepali heavy-fleet operator defines and records service, not against observed daily use. We accept this as the explicit, PO-decided cost of the phase pull-forward — and it is reversible in the sense that an *unaccepted* ADR ships nothing.
- **A hard dependency on a not-yet-accepted ADR (ADR-0036).** The engine-hours dimension — the reason this is worth pulling forward — cannot be built until ADR-0036 is accepted and its slice ships. We accept the ordering constraint and the risk that ADR-0036 changes shape at its own acceptance (the engine-hours trigger this consumes is verified against ADR-0036's final form before the PM slice builds; see "Revisit when").
- **The badge is passive (Phase-3 delivery deferred).** Like the compliance badge, it warns only when the operator opens the Vehicle detail page; an asset whose page is never viewed will not surface its overdue service until DEC3/ADR-0038 adds the active reminder channel. We accept this by design — building the passive producer now and the delivery channel in its proper phase keeps later-phase scope from leaking in.
- **A denormalized anchor that must be kept honest.** Storing `lastService*` on the schedule (rather than always deriving from the latest `ServiceRecord`) trades a small staleness risk for read performance — the same trade `Vehicle.odometerCurrentKm` already makes — and is mitigated by advancing it in the same transaction as the record write.

## Revisit when

- **PO acceptance fixes the open picks.** Acceptance ratifies the storage shape (the recommended dedicated `ServiceSchedule` + `ServiceRecord` aggregate, versus columns on `Vehicle`), the per-dimension "due soon" windows (the km / engine-hours / days thresholds, the analog of ADR-0031's 30-day compliance window — the PO's to set), the cost-link delete posture (`Restrict` vs. `SetNull`), and the `ServiceType`-enum-vs-free-text and `ScheduleStatus` recommendations. If the PO chooses differently, the implementing slice follows the PO's pick and this ADR is annotated.
- **The PO rules on the phase pull-forward and the ADR-0036 ordering.** Acceptance is where the PO decides whether the PM slice proceeds now (after/with ADR-0036), waits for the production deploy / Phase 3, is scoped to km+calendar only until ADR-0036 lands, or is held entirely (see "Phase mismatch"). An undeployed Phase 1 carrying two pulled-forward Phase-3 aggregates is the ADR-0025 "built-but-unused" risk; if it accumulates, that is the signal the deploy must precede further pull-forwards.
- **ADR-0036 is accepted or changes shape.** Confirm the engine-hours trigger (the `meterType` discriminator, the `engineHoursCurrent` column, the integer-tenths scale) matches what the `ENGINE_HOURS` interval type consumes; adjust commitment 2/3 if ADR-0036's final form differs from its proposal.
- **DEC3/ADR-0038 (reminder delivery) is designed.** Confirm the "service due/overdue" state this aggregate produces is the shape the notification channel and the "reminder delivery" SLI want to read; the on-demand badge may gain a background daily scan there.
- **Operators want fleet-template schedules** (define a service plan once, apply to every like-model asset). The trigger to revisit the per-vehicle-only v1 scope toward a reusable schedule template.
- **An asset needs a third meter dimension** (operating cycles, liters pumped, drum-rotations) as a service interval — the trigger to revisit whether the typed `intervalType` enum generalizes cleanly or whether a richer model is warranted, the same question ADR-0036 parked for meter readings.
- **Parts, labor, or technician assignment become real needs.** If maintenance grows beyond interval-scheduling + cost-linked history toward a full CMMS, that is its own decision; this aggregate is deliberately the smaller thing.
